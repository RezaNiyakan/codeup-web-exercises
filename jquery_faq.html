10 Facts about National Parks

Grand Canyon
The Grand Canyon, in Grand Canyon National Park in Arizona, is known as one of the Seven Natural Wonders of the World.
Everglades
Everglades National Park protects more than 25% of Florida's original everglades (subtropical wetlands).
Yellowstone
Yellowstone National Park (in Wyoming, Idaho, and Montana) is the oldest U.S. national park, founded in 1872.
Acres
In the National Park System, there are 407 sites covering more than 84 million acres of land.
Applachian Trail
The Appalachian Trail is a 2,185 mile long public footpath that traverses the scenic, wooded, pastoral, wild, and culturally resonant lands of the Appalachian Mountains.
Foot Traffic
In 2014, more than 290 million people visited the National Parks.
Carlsbad Caverns
Carlsbad Caverns National Park in New Mexico is home to the nation's deepest cave, which is 1,593 feet deep.
Crater Lake
At 1,932 feet deep, Crater Lake National Park in Oregon is the deepest lake in the U.S.
Mount Rainier
Mount Rainier is the most glaciated peak in the contiguous U.S. with 26 major glaciers.
Hot Springs National Park
Originally established by Congress as Hot Springs Reservation in 1832 and later becoming a national park in 1921, Hot Springs National Park represents the oldest protected area in the National Park System.

Sequoia National Park

  • Located in Visalia, California.
  • The park has over 800 miles in trails.
  • The first inhabitants known to have inhabited the area now designated as Sequoia National Park were the Monache Indians.
  • Has the largest living single-stem tree in the world, the wonderfully named General Sherman. The tree is approximately 275 feet tall and weighs approximately 1,900 metric tons.

Badlands

  • Located in South Dakota.
  • The Badlands are a collection of buttes, pinnacles, spires, and mixed-grass prairies.
  • The wildlife includes bison, bighorn sheep, black-footed ferrets, and prairie dogs.
  • The White River Badlands contain the largest assemblage of known late Eocene and Oligocene mammal fossils.

Death Valley

  • Death Valley is home to Badwater Basin, which has the lowest elevation in North America.
  • This park contains canyons, badlands, sand dunes, and mountain ranges.
  • Historic mines can be located here as well.
  • Has the hottest, lowest and driest location in the US.
